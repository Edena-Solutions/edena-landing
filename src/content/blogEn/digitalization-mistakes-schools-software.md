<strong>Digitization in schools: 7 mistakes that schools make when implementing educational software</strong>

<br>

Educational digitalization is advancing, but many centers make mistakes that delay results, generate resistance or waste investment. Well-chosen and implemented school software transforms management; poorly implemented, it becomes a burden. These are the 7 most common mistakes in digitalization in schools and how to avoid them.

<br>

<strong>1. Choosing software for schools without defining real needs</strong>

<br>

Buy "to be up to date" without mapping the processes that fail the most. Effective educational digitization begins by identifying bottlenecks: billing, attendance, communication, records. Software for schools must solve specific problems, not be a technological whim.

<br>

<strong>2. Do not involve the secretariat and administration</strong>

<br>

The decision is made only by management or IT, and the team that will use the system does not participate. Digitalization in schools fails when the secretariat and administration have not tested the tool or given feedback. Software for schools must be usable by those who will use it every day.

<br>

<strong>3. Underestimate data migration</strong>

<br>

Assume that "the data passes itself" or that you can start from scratch without loss. Educational digitization requires planning the migration: what data is critical, what format it is in, who validates it. Software for schools that does not contemplate migration creates chaos when changing.

<br>

<strong>4. Do not plan training or adoption</strong>

<br>

Deploy software for schools and expect the team to "learn as they go." Digitalization in schools requires training, documentation and adaptation time. Without this, resistance grows and the system is only half used.

<br>

<strong>5. Choose by price without evaluating total cost</strong>

<br>

The license is only one part. Training, migration, integrations and support add up. Cheap educational digitization can be expensive if the software for schools does not cover what is necessary or requires many patches. Compare total cost at 3 years.

<br>

<strong>6. Ignore data protection and regulations</strong>

<br>

Signing with a provider without reviewing GDPR, data location or data processor clauses. Digitization in schools involves sensitive data; Software for schools must comply with regulations. Don't assume that "anything goes."

<br>

<strong>7. Do not measure results or adjust

<br>

Implant and forget. Educational digitalization is a continuous process. Software for schools must allow impact to be measured (time saved, reduced defaults, satisfaction) and adjustments. Without measurement, you don't know if the investment is worth it.

<br>

<strong>How to avoid these mistakes in digitalization in schools</strong>

<br>

Define needs, involve the team, plan migration and training, assess total cost, demand regulatory compliance and measure results. Well-chosen and implemented school software transforms the school; Avoid these 7 mistakes and maximize your return.

<br>

<strong>Seven mistakes we see repeated</strong>

<br>

- **Implement in September without pilot:** Without a test in June or a limited module, the enrollment peak collapses, secretariat.
- **Buy for initial price:** 36-month TCO and internal hours usually reverse the ranking.
- **Without internal responsibility:** External IT without management mandate does not prioritize adoption.
- **Excel in indefinite parallel:** Two sources of truth perpetuate errors and hidden lateness.
- **Ignore training by role:** Secretariat formed but tutors lost generates rejection of change.
- **Do not measure before/after:** Without starting KPI there is no pilot cut-off criterion.
- **No similar references:** Academy software does not fit in schools with preschool or NEAE.

<br>

<strong>How to avoid them</strong>

<br>

- **Limited pilot:** June or a module (charges or admission) with real data before the global cut-off.
- **TCO at 36 months:** License, migration, training and integrations in the same unit.
- **Biweekly Committee:** Management, secretariat and stage reference with a fixed agenda.
- **Legacy cut-off date:** Excel or old tool with a calendar purpose, not "coexistence".
- **Agreed KPI:** Admin hours, delinquency or enrollment time with written success threshold.<br>

<strong>Context in Spain: what a school ERP requires in 2026</strong>

<br>

Beyond grades and attendance, school management software in Spain must cover billing (including Verifactu preparation), family portal or app, file with minor data under GDPR and LOPDGDD, and communications traceability. Buying an isolated SIS when the real pain is in secretarial, collections and families usually ends in duplicating data between three tools.

In nursery schools they weigh places, ratios, menus and allergies; in academies, bonuses, seasons and multiple venues; in private schools, differentiated fees, internal scholarships and commercial recruitment. An ERP that works in language academies may fall short if you manage child, NEAE or dining room ratios with billing per day.

Evaluates three-year TCO: license, migration, training, internal coordination hours and cost of bridge integrations. Request a demo with your real data (five families, one cycle of receipts) before deciding. Software that seems cheaper at the beginning can become more expensive if it forces you to add CRM, billing or communication separately.

<br>

<strong>Case study (Spain)</strong>

<br>

An 85-place nursery school in Girona evaluated three providers with the same checklist: enrollment, menus, billing and family app. The 10-week pilot with real data revealed that only one closed the application-enrollment-payment cycle without Excel. The change cost them 40 hours of migration, but they eliminated duplicate data in three tools.
