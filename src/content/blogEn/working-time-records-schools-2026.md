<strong>Working-time records in schools: obligations and how to comply in 2026</strong>

<br>

Since 2019, every educational centre in Spain with employed staff — private and state-subsidised schools, nurseries, academies — has been required to record its team's daily working hours. Many still solve it with sign-in sheets in the staff room or a spreadsheet someone fills in at the end of the month: exactly the kind of record a labour inspection dismantles in five minutes. This article reviews what the rule actually demands, what the non-compliant centre risks, and how to run time tracking without adding bureaucracy to a team already short of hours.

<br>

<strong>What the rule requires</strong>

<br>

Royal Decree-Law 8/2019 requires recording each worker's day, every day, with the specific start and end times. Records must be kept for four years and remain available to workers, their representatives and the Labour Inspectorate. The theoretical timetable in the contract is not enough: what gets recorded is the day actually worked, including variations. And during an inspection visit, the centre must be able to produce the records there and then, not reconstruct them later.

<br>

<strong>Who it covers in an educational centre</strong>

<br>

All employed staff: teachers in private and state-subsidised education, nursery educators, academy teachers, administrative and services staff, canteen and after-school monitors. The teaching day has its quirks — it combines teaching hours, non-teaching hours, cover duties and meetings — but that does not exempt it from the record: it makes it more necessary, because it is where real hours drift away from theoretical ones most easily without anyone documenting it.

<br>

<strong>What the centre risks</strong>

<br>

- Fines of up to 7,500 euros for a serious infringement in working-time records.
- A presumption of unpaid overtime when the working day is undocumented, with its contributions.
- Labour disputes that are hard to defend without reliable records.
- Management time consumed reconstructing records after a requirement.

The risk is not theoretical: working-time records are among the Labour Inspectorate's most routine checks, precisely because they are easy to verify. Either the records exist and add up, or they do not.

<br>

<strong>Why paper and spreadsheets end up failing</strong>

<br>

The sign-in sheet works for the first month. Then come the omissions, the block signatures on Friday, the spreadsheet filled in from memory and the weeks nobody remembered at all. The result is a record that exists but does not protect: no traceability, easy to challenge and costly to maintain. Spanish regulation is also clearly trending towards digital records with remote access for the Inspectorate, so the investment in digitising clock-ins is not just about complying today — it is about not having to switch systems again tomorrow.

<br>

<strong>How it should work in a school</strong>

<br>

Good time tracking for a school or nursery looks nothing like a factory turnstile. Staff clock in and out with one tap from their phone or browser; records are stored with their exact time and cannot be edited retroactively; a missed clock-in triggers an automatic alert instead of leaving a gap; and management can export hours per employee and period whenever the payroll adviser or the Inspectorate asks. If the clock-in also lives alongside the school agenda — cover duties, substitutions, meetings — in the same tool, the record stops being a parallel chore and becomes part of the routine. <strong>Context in Spain: from paper to traceability</strong>

<br>

The working-time registry started out generic, but its application to education has been sharpening: collective agreements in private and subsidised education that detail complex working days, more frequent inspections and a labour administration going digital. At the same time, school leadership teams have ever less tolerance for added bureaucracy: the only sustainable way to comply is for clocking in to cost one second and reports to generate themselves.
