<strong>FUNDAE Subsidies: How to Manage Them in Training Academies</strong>

<br>

For an academy that delivers training to companies, the FUNDAE subsidy is both an enormous commercial lever and a source of administrative headaches. It is a lever because it lets the client company recover much of the cost through its training credit, making the sale much easier to close. And it is a headache because the subsidy depends on impeccable documentation: an incomplete attendance record, a wrongly entered piece of data, or a communication out of deadline can leave your client unable to claim the subsidy. This article explains, without promising miracles, what subsidized training is, which documentation is critical, and how to manage it in an academy so the administrative side stops being the bottleneck.

<br>

<strong>What subsidized training is and who puts up the money</strong>

<br>

Subsidized training —or training programmed by companies— is that which a company organizes for its workers and can subsidize through the training credit available in its contributions, within the FUNDAE framework. The most confused point: the academy does not receive money from FUNDAE. The academy invoices its training service to the company, and it is the company that applies the subsidy in its social security if the training meets the requirements. The academy is the piece that organizes, delivers, and above all documents so that subsidy is possible.

<br>

<strong>The academy's role: the entity that makes the subsidy possible</strong>

<br>

Many academies act as the organizing or delivering entity of their corporate clients' training. That makes the quality of their document management part of the product: a company doesn't just buy a course, it buys the peace of mind of being able to subsidize it without scares. That's why academies that master FUNDAE management have a real competitive advantage: they can offer subsidized training with process guarantees, not just content. And that's why an administrative slip is not just an internal problem: it erodes client trust.

<br>

<strong>The critical documentation that can't fail</strong>

<br>

- Attendance and participation control, complete and traceable per session.
- Correct data of each participant and of the client company.
- Dates, duration, and characteristics of the training action.
- Compliance with the deadlines for communicating actions.
- Orderly retention of the entire file in case of a check.

It's worth insisting: the specific requirements and deadlines are set by current regulations and should always be confirmed with your accountant. What does not change is that documentation is the point where subsidies are won or lost.

<br>

<strong>Why well-planned subsidies are lost</strong>

<br>

Most lost subsidies don't fail because of the training: they fail because of administration. An attendance sheet with gaps, a participant with a mistyped ID, a date that doesn't match what was communicated, or a communication sent out of deadline. They are small errors with big consequences, and almost all share the same origin: managing the file on paper and scattered spreadsheets, reconstructing it at the last minute. When data lives in order from day one, those errors stop appearing.

<br>

<strong>No software "guarantees" the subsidy (and whoever promises it is lying)</strong>

<br>

It must be said clearly: no software guarantees the FUNDAE subsidy. The subsidy depends on regulatory compliance, correct delivery, and on-time processing. What a good management system does is organize the work that underpins the subsidy: participants, attendance, dates, communication with the company, and documentation, with traceability. The difference is not "comply for you," it's "reach the moment of justification with everything in order" instead of searching for papers. That difference, multiplied by each training action, is hours and is risk.
